Problems with the Paycheck Protection Program

Problems with the PPP include unjust distribution of resource, tax avoidance, fraud, and undemocratic control of taxpayer funds.

Forgiving a PPP loan is a Handout to the Rich

The Paycheck Protection Program forgives loans and owners get to pocket the cash. Skeptics may respond that they had to use the money to pay their employees – you know, to “protect” the paychecks. However, workers had to labor, hours and hours each day for months, to get a paycheck. When the loan is forgiven, nothing changes for those workers. Indeed, their work likely contributed to the profit of the business owner. Many companies likely saw no downturn in revenue and their profit margins may have expanded. In those cases owners benefit in multiple ways. First, they get to keep the profits from the business. Second, they get to pocket that sweet PPP cash.

A Foregiven Loan Equals Free Money

Imagine I loaned you $5 at some point and said you had to use it on something that you were already spending money on, like food. Then, you bought some food and I said, “Your loan is forgiven.” That is the precise mathematical equivalent of just giving you $5. Sure, I said you could only buy food with it, but your bank account increased by $5. The PPP gave out more than $500 billion in loans. That’s a lot of money to just give out to business owners.

Republicans Fought for Favorable Tax Treatment of PPP Reciepients

A forgiven loan is essentially unearned income – the business owner received cash in exchange for no work. That scenario is quite different workers at that business who must sell their labor in exchange for a paycheck. Indeed, if workers were paid during the pandemic, their income was subject to income tax. However, when owners were given income through the PPP (a forgiven loan is income, it is “free” money!), they might not have to pay tax.

The Internal Revenue Service has hinted that it will tax income from forgiven PPP loans, although Republican members of Congress attempted in Dec. 2020 to create new legislation that would prevent the IRS from taxing that unearned income.

Big is Small: Loopholes that benefitted large corporations

Here’s some choice language from the CARES Act, which launched the Paycheck Protection program:

“During the covered period, any business concern that employs not more than 500 employees per physical location of the business concern and that is assigned a North American Industry Classification System code beginning with 72 at the time of disbursal shall be eligible to receive a covered loan.”

The 72 refers to businesses in the “Accommodation and Food Services” industry. So, huge restaurants, hotels, casinos, RV parks and campgrounds, mobile food services and more were eligible. Mind you, 500 employees in a single physical location for a restaurant would make it an enormous business. I rarely, if ever eat in a restaurant that seats 500 people – here, we are talking 500 employees. Moreover, just 500 in a single physical location. So it may be the case that a company with 100,000 employees could get a loan as long as it has 200 locations.

Big Publicaly Traded Corporations Received “Small Business” Loans

The market capitalization of Fuel Cell Inc. was $4.8 billion dollars as of early 2021, yet the corporation recieved a forgivable loan of $6.5 from the American taxpayer. See the SEC filing here.

Fuel Cell wasn’t the only one. Shake Shack applied for a loan but decided against it after public pressure. According to the company’s SEC filing, the largest chain of sports clubs in the North East is Town Sports International, with 8,000 employees. They also have sites in Switzerland. The “small” corporation received a 2.7 million loan courtesy of the PPP.

Low Interest Rates

The annual interest rate for loans through the PPP is 1%. The average interest rate for a home mortgage loan in Jan. 2020 is approx. 2.1%. This is the lowest it has been since at least 1971. Moreover, mortgage loans with that low of an interest rate would nearly always require collateral, something which is not required in the PPP. Other types of loans (e.g., business loans) almost always have higher interest rates. I don’t think the federal government should charge high interest rates for loans, but some loans will default and the taxpayer will be the ultimate loser. With higher interest rates across all loans the taxpayer could recoup some of the losses.

No Collatoral was Provided for Loans

If you want a home or car loan, the bank will often require collateral – that is, you are required to put up the home or car as security to the bank. This is why you don’t really own a home if you have a mortgage. If you don’t pay back the loan in cash, the bank gets the collateral. In the case of more than $500 billion in loans given to the rich through the PPP, no collateral was provided. Some of the 5 million business owners who received loans will default from bad business decisions or simply take the money and run. With no collateral, the US taxpayer will foot the entire bill.

Banks Benefit Big

The SBA pays banks for processing PPP loans. For example, for a $2 million loan, a bank will receive $20,000. For processing a $350,000 loan a bank would receive $17,500. An analysis from Wall Street Journal in July, 2020 found that banks could recieve $24 billion in fees. These banks include JP Morgan Chase & Co. and Bank of America Corp, which means some of the biggest banks will benefit big from a Small Business Administration program supposedly aimed to help “small” businesses.
